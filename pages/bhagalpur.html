A City Written in History

Bhagalpur derives its name from Bhagdatpuram — meaning "City of Good Luck." Historically known as Champa Nagari, it was the capital of the ancient Anga Mahajanapada (6th century BCE), referenced in both the Ramayana and the Mahabharata. The city flourished under the Gupta Empire, the Pala dynasty, and the Mughal era before becoming a significant administrative centre during British rule.

Chinese travellers Fa-Hien and Hieun-Tsang recorded Bhagalpur as an indispensable trade centre in eastern India during the 7th century — one of the few Indian cities documented in both ancient Indian texts and foreign historical records.

Vikramshila — Where Knowledge Lived

Established by Emperor Dharmapala of the Pala Empire in the 8th century CE, Vikramshila Mahavihara was one of the greatest centres of Buddhist learning in the ancient world — rivalling Nalanda University at its peak. It drew scholars from across Asia and focused on multiple fields including Tantricism, logic, and philosophy. It was destroyed by Bakhtiyar Khilji in 1203 CE. Today, the ruins at Antichak village in Bhagalpur district are a protected archaeological site, and Vikramshila Central University is currently under construction nearby.

The Silk City — A GI-Tagged Legacy

Bhagalpur's Tussar silk is one of India's most iconic fabrics — internationally recognized for its natural lustre, robustness, and airy texture. The silk industry here is over 200 years old, supporting more than 35,000 weavers on 25,000 looms. In 2013, Bhagalpur silk was granted Geographical Indication (GI) status — the same protection as Darjeeling tea and Basmati rice. It now reaches markets in West Asia, Europe, the US, and Japan.

The Ganga & the Gangetic Dolphin

The Ganga flows along the southern edge of the city — shaping its agriculture, trade, spiritual culture, and daily life. The river around Bhagalpur is one of the last habitats of the Gangetic dolphin, the national aquatic animal of India. The Vikramshila Gangetic Dolphin Sanctuary is established here — a protected zone that makes Bhagalpur one of the most important conservation sites in the country.

Bhagalpur is also one of only three places in the world — along with Cambodia and Assam — where the endangered Greater Adjutant Stork (Garuda bird) nests and breeds, growing from 78 to over 500 birds since 2007.

Culture, Language & Festivals

The Angika culture defines the cultural identity of this region. The city blends Hindu, Muslim, Buddhist, and Jain traditions across its history and present life.

Kali Puja

The most important festival; Shobhayatra celebrated its 71st year in 2024. The post-puja procession takes 36 hours to complete — one of the longest in Bihar.

Bishari Puja (Manasa)

A folk festival originating from Champapuri, ancient capital of Anga Mahajanapada.

Major Festivals

Chhath Puja, Durga Puja, Eid, and Shravani Mela.

Art Forms

Manjusha paintings, Madhubani paintings, Kashida handloom work, terracotta crafts, and Chhau dance.
